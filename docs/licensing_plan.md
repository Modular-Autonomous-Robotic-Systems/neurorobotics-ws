Community driven and open development of intellectual property provides a sustainable means of democratising knowledge legally. The open source licensing framework provides certain rights and protections in lieu of bearing certain responsibilities to each benefactor of and contributor to open source work. For any individual or organisation to distribute or redistribute `copyleft` open source IP as is or with modifications, they must pass on the same rights, protections and responsibilities vested onto them through the licenses their works bear. Permissive open source IP may be redistributed without any liability. Thus, if one wishes to integrate open source IP into their work or claim their work to be open source, they must provide the source required to freely redistribute the work  and relay the rights, protections and responsibilities granted under the open source license covering the work to the recipient of their work, if the license requires so.

A for-profit entity who wishes to operate in the open source domain, however, must understand and navigate the legal framework of the open source ecosystem with a nuanced comprehension of the rights, protections and responsibilities granted to the benefactors of and the contributors to open source work. From hereinafter we will refer to both benefactors of and contributors to open source work as members of the community of the open source work unless a distinction is made. As a member of the open source community, a for profit entity must provide the source of the work derivative of open source work or claiming to be open source work, to the recipients of the work. Such a responsibility may entail significant friction in letting such a for-profit entity maintain relevance. This is especially true in the case of hardware centric intellectual property. In a competitive world free distribution of distinguishing features of products can lead to significant losses. However, the allure of community driven development, large scale adoption, quick testing and feedback times and R&D cost reduction make operating in the open source ecosystem lucrative for frugal organisations. This is especially true for resource intensive R&D in a resource lacking environment. Entities generally integrate properietary IP with an open source core to maintain a distinguishing brand to capture and maintain their market share. Examples of such entities are BeagleBone, Raspberry Pi, RISC V, Arduino, Linux, Android, Terraform etc. Each entity has adopted a unique strategy to build a moat in their respective ecosystems to become indisposable.

Before delving into the strategy for `The Neuro-Robotics Toolkit` for operating within the open source ecosystem, let us understand the primary license categories relevant to the project. The following are the four relevant licensing frameworks are relevant to the project:
1. **Permissive Licenses** This category of licenses allows for free redistribution of protected work without passing on the obligation of keeping modifications open source. These licenses essentially allow free use of protected work. Apache 2.0, BSD 3-Clause, CERN-OSHW-P and CC-BY, fall under this category
2. **Copyleft Licenses** Licenses such as GPL v3, CERN-OSHW-S and CC-BY-SA fall under this category. These licenses allow free redistribution of protected work, however, they require relaying of the rights, protections and responsibilities granted by the license to the recipients of the work. It must be noted that use of work protected by such licenses does not necessitate releasing of modifications or the source of derivative work protected under such licenses to the public, but only to the recipient of the work.
3. **Source Available Licenses** Licenses such as CC-BY-SA-NC and BUSL 1.1 fall under this category. These licenses are not considered truly open source as they restrict commercial redistribution of protected work. Such licenses are often accompanied by clauses promising release under truly open source licenses after the lapse of a specified period of time, generally set to recuperate investment costs of the protected work.
4. **Proprietary Licenses** Trademarks, Trade Secrets and other protective licenses meant to prevent undesired access to protected IP. These are used to maintain a competitive edge by maintaining secret IP.

In addition to aforementioned, open source projects use `Contributor Licensing Agreements` to gain re-licensing rights of open source contributions to protect themselves from unwanted litigation when commercialising open core components of their work. Such licenses however, do not give explicit permissions to contributing members of the project ecosystem to freely use the protected work without revealing the source of modifications if copyleft licenses are used.

We also need to understand that copyleft licenses allow for commercial use of protected work under the following circumstances:
1. The source of modifications to the protected work are to be provided to the recipient of the work
2. In the case of software deployed on protected hardware, the distributing entity must provide access to update and modify protected work on the hardware
3. In case of using software components licensed differently, protected software must not be linked to proprietary work and must only communicate through open IPC protocols such as DDS, MQT, Kafka etc.
4. In case of use protected hardware, updating through add-ons or component updates do not require sharing of source of the components. The modifications must be such that the existing IP remains unchanged and proprietary IP is added on top of the protected hardware. There must be provisions to replace the proprietary IP with open source IP through standard interfaces.
5. Closed source re-licensing of protected work is only permissible either through consent from all contributors or the primary party of `Contributor Licensing Agreements` owning the project. It must be noted however, that closed sourced re-licensing of third party open source protected work requires licensing from the third party under any circumstances.
The Open Source strategy of `The Neuro-Robotics Toolkit` rests on the appropriate application of the aforementioned distribution constraints. The architecture of the toolkit and products designed using the same are to be designed and maintained based on the aforementioned principles.

To establish a defensible moat to maintain relevance and competitive edge, `The Neuro-Robotics Toolkit` adopts a modular approach to both hardware and software. To comply with the requirements of using open source while building proprietary IP, the toolkit components are divided into three categories:
1. **Copyleft Protected Components** These are the GPLv3, CERN-OSHW-S and CC-BY-SA protected IP. These are the open source work which require explicit reciprocal rights conveyance for legal permissions to use the work. In case of software, such work must not be linked to proprietary work to prevent infection. IPC communication and standalone usage with means for updation and modification are enough to comply with such licenses. In case of hardware, proprietary components may be integrated or the IP integrated into proprietary systems through standardised methods which should allow for easy removal and modification of open sourced components. 
2. **Open Components** These are the Apache 2.0, BSD 3-Clause, CC-BY, and CERN-OSHW-P licensed work. These may be integrated or redistributed freely. The open source software middleware is required for communication between proprietary and copyleft protected IP. In case of hardware, such IP may be used to link proprietary and open components
3. **Closed/Source Available Components** These components form the proprietary core of the project and will not be freely available for commercial use. Such IP may be licensed through CC-BY-SA-NC, BUSL 1.1, Trademarks, Trade Secrets etc. Such work will have to be architecturally protected from open source components to ensure no licensing infection occurs.

The aforementioned guidelines form the core open source and commercialisation strategy of `The Neuro-Robotics Toolkit`. Let us consider the application of the aforementioned principles to a few case studies of possible systems that can be built using `The Neuro-Robotics Toolkit`. To illustrate the practical application of the aforementioned licensing principles, we consider three distinct operational use cases: 
1. A fixed-wing kamikaze drone
2. A military quadruped designed for patrol and combat
3. An Unmanned Ground Vehicle (UGV) equipped with a robotic manipulator for explosive ordnance disposal (EOD). 

For each system, the architectural components are bifurcated into hardware and software domains, and subsequently categorized by their licensing strategy: Copyleft Protected, Permissive (Open), and Proprietary (Closed/Source Available). This structural separation ensures that the core open-source components remain legally compliant and community-driven, while the proprietary augmentations that fulfill stringent military requirements and provide a competitive edge remain safeguarded.

Crucially, the hardware architecture relies on permissive electrical and mechanical interfaces to decouple the open-source components from proprietary modules, ensuring that proprietary components (e.g., advanced sensing, ruggedization, encrypted communication, cyber-security patches etc.) can be seamlessly replaced by Commercial Off-The-Shelf (COTS) alternatives without licensing infection. Similarly, the software architecture utilizes permissive middleware (such as ROS 2 or MQTT) as an Inter-Process Communication (IPC) boundary, isolating copyleft algorithms (like ORB-SLAM3) from proprietary, task-specific operational logic.

In order to maintain the flexibility required to commercialize open-core components as discussed in the case studies above, projects operating within this framework often employ Contributor Licensing Agreements (CLAs). A CLA ensures that while the community retains the right to use and modify the open-source base under its original license, the overarching entity retains the necessary re-licensing rights to incorporate contributions into proprietary offerings safely. This legal mechanism protects the project from intellectual property disputes and provides a clear pathway for commercial sustainability without violating the rights of individual contributors. `The Neuro-Robotics Toolkit` employs CLAs to protect the project owners' rights to re-license the open-core as per commercial requirements. This CLA is to be drafted along with appropriate proprietary licenses.
